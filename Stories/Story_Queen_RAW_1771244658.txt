# Cult: The Porcelain Empress



### The Invisible Woman

# THE PORCELAIN EMPRESS

## Chapter 1: The Invisible Woman

The coffee in the break room had been sitting on the burner for three hours, thick as motor oil and twice as bitter. Zelda Blackwood poured herself a cup anyway, watching the viscous liquid crawl into her chipped mug—the one with "World's Best Secretary!" printed in fading letters that nobody had meant sincerely even when they'd given it to her six years ago.

Through the glass partition, she could see Mr. Hendricks's office. Could see the back of Amber Reeves's blonde head as she perched on the edge of his mahogany desk, one tanned leg crossed over the other, her pencil skirt riding up just enough. Twenty-six years old. Fresh. Eager. Everything Zelda had been when she'd started at Hendricks & Associates two decades ago, before the gray had crept into her temples and gravity had begun its patient work on her body.

The intercom on her desk crackled. "Zelda, could you come in here, please?"

She set down her coffee. Smoothed her sensible navy skirt. Checked her reflection in the dark computer screen—nothing to fix, nothing remarkable, just the same forgettable face that had stared back at her for forty years.

Hendricks's office smelled like leather and expensive cologne. Amber was still perched on that desk, but now Hendricks stood behind it, his hands clasped in that pose he adopted when he was about to deliver bad news and pretend it was good.

"Zelda, thank you for coming in." His smile was professional, distant. "Please, sit."

She sat in the chair opposite his desk—the supplicant's chair, lower than his by design. Amber examined her manicure, a small smile playing at the corners of her glossed lips.

"I've been reviewing our organizational structure," Hendricks continued, "and I think it's time we made some changes. You've been an invaluable member of our team for—how long has it been?"

"Twenty-two years this October."

"Twenty-two years! Remarkable dedication. Which is why I know you'll understand that it's time for us to evolve. To bring fresh perspectives to the executive floor." He gestured to Amber, who finally looked up, that smile widening. "Amber has shown exceptional initiative these past six months. Her organizational skills, her client relations—"

The skills Zelda had taught her. Every shortcut, every preference, every trick for managing Hendricks's demanding schedule.

"—so I'm promoting her to Senior Executive Assistant. She'll be taking over your current portfolio."

The fluorescent lights hummed. Somewhere down the hall, a phone rang and rang.

"And my position?" Zelda's voice came out smaller than she'd intended.

"You'll remain as Administrative Support. General filing, document preparation, that sort of thing. Same salary, of course. We value your experience."

Same salary. Different title. A demotion wrapped in corporate speak and delivered with a smile that didn't reach Hendricks's eyes. Amber was looking at her now, that expression equal parts pity and triumph.

Zelda stood. "I understand."

She didn't trust herself to say more.

—

The studio apartment was dark when she returned at 6:47 PM, the precise time she'd returned every weeknight for the past eleven years. She didn't turn on the lights. Just stood in the doorway, her keys still dangling from her hand, staring at the efficiency kitchen that bled into the sleeping area that pretended to be separate from the living space.

Four hundred square feet of existence.

Her phone buzzed. A text from her sister Sarah: *"Another nephew on the way! #4! Want to come to the gender reveal party?"*

Zelda set the phone face-down on the counter. Poured herself a glass of wine from the bottle she'd opened three days ago. Sat on the futon that served as both couch and bed, staring at nothing.

Forty years old. Never married. No children. No prospects. No career trajectory except down. She'd been careful, responsible, saving herself for the right moment, the right person, the right opportunity. But there was no right moment. Just this expanding emptiness where a life should be.

The wine was sour on her tongue.

She opened her laptop—refurbished, three years old—and typed without thinking: *women's groups near me*.

The search results bloomed across her screen. Book clubs. Networking events. Divorced women's support groups. She scrolled, her finger hovering over the touchpad, until something made her pause.

**LUMINESCENCE: Where Women Become Light**

The website was elegant, minimalist. Soft-focus images of women laughing, embracing, radiating confidence. No prices listed. No cheesy testimonials. Just a simple intake form and an address in the Gallery District—the part of downtown where Zelda never went, where old warehouses had been converted into lofts she couldn't afford.

*"Luminescence is an invitation-only collective for women seeking authentic transformation. We don't promise quick fixes. We promise truth."*

Her cursor hovered over the contact form. This was stupid. She was tired, drunk on cheap wine and self-pity. These things were always pyramid schemes or glorified therapy sessions where suburban women complained about their comfortable lives.

But her fingers were already typing. Name. Age. Email. And in the box marked "What are you searching for?": *I don't want to be invisible anymore.*

She hit submit before she could stop herself.

The confirmation screen appeared instantly: *"Thank you, Zelda. Someone will contact you within 24 hours."*

She closed the laptop. Drained her wine. Told herself she wouldn't answer when they called.

But even as the thought formed, she knew she was lying.

### The Threshold

# THE PORCELAIN EMPRESS

## Chapter 2: The Threshold

The Gallery District looked different after dark. Zelda's Honda Civic crept past converted warehouses where exposed brick glowed honey-warm behind industrial windows. Beautiful people stood outside wine bars, their laughter carrying on the October air. She checked the address on her phone three times, certain she'd made a mistake.

The building had no sign. Just a number etched in brass beside a recessed doorway that whispered money in a language Zelda barely spoke. She sat in her car for seven minutes, watching the entrance, waiting for someone else to arrive so she wouldn't have to enter alone.

No one came.

Her phone buzzed: *"We're expecting you, Zelda. Third floor. The door will open."*

She hadn't told them she'd arrived.

The lobby was temperature-controlled perfection, all polished concrete and strategic lighting. A single elevator waited, its doors already open. Zelda's sensible flats whispered against the floor as she stepped inside. The doors closed with a pneumatic sigh, and the elevator rose in silence broken only by her own breathing.

Third floor. The doors opened onto a space that shouldn't exist—a vast loft with twenty-foot ceilings, where white silk curtains divided the room into mysterious chambers. Candles flickered in glass hurricanes. Something that smelled like jasmine and smoke curled through the air.

"Zelda Blackwood."

The voice came from everywhere and nowhere. Then a woman emerged from behind one of the silk partitions, and Zelda's breath caught.

She was tall—nearly six feet in heels that could have been weapons. Mid-forties, perhaps, with silver threading through dark hair she wore pulled back in a severe bun. Her dress was simple black, high-necked, long-sleeved, but it moved against her body like liquid. No jewelry except a single platinum ring on her right index finger.

But it was her eyes that stopped Zelda's heart. Dark and fathomless and completely certain.

"I'm Vivienne Calloway." She extended one hand, palm down, as though Zelda might kiss it. The gesture felt archaic and absolute. "Welcome to Luminescence."

Zelda shook her hand instead, feeling Vivienne's cool fingers wrap around hers with surprising strength. "Thank you for seeing me. I wasn't sure if—"

"If you'd be accepted?" A smile ghosted across Vivienne's face. "We're very selective. But your application intrigued me." She released Zelda's hand and gestured toward the white curtains. "Come. Let me show you what we offer."

Beyond the first partition, three women sat in a circle on floor cushions. They looked up as Vivienne led Zelda past—women in their thirties and forties, dressed in simple white robes that looked expensive even in candlelight. One of them, a redhead with laugh lines around her eyes, smiled at Zelda with something like recognition.

"These are some of our Initiates," Vivienne said. "Women who came to us lost, diminished, invisible." She paused, turning to face Zelda fully. "Women like you."

The words should have stung. Instead, they landed with the weight of truth.

Another partition, another space. Here, mirrors lined one wall, reflecting candlelight into infinity. A massage table draped in pristine white sheets occupied the center. Glass bottles filled with amber and violet liquids lined a shelf.

"Tell me," Vivienne said, her voice dropping lower, more intimate. "What do you see when you look in a mirror?"

Zelda's reflection stared back at her—forty years of playing small, dressing sensible, making herself forgettable. The gray in her temples. The lines around her mouth from never quite smiling. The body she'd learned to hide under shapeless blazers and modest hemlines.

"Nothing special," she heard herself say.

"Precisely." Vivienne moved behind her, both their reflections now captured in the mirror. "You've been taught to disappear. To serve. To make yourself convenient for others' comfort." Her hands came to rest on Zelda's shoulders—not quite touching, hovering just above the fabric of her blazer. "Do you know what I see?"

Zelda couldn't speak.

"Potential." Vivienne's breath was warm against her ear. "You're forty years old. You think your value has diminished. But men—the right kind of men—they don't want girls. Girls are everywhere, cheap, forgettable. They want women who understand power. Women who've lived enough to know what they possess."

"I don't understand what you mean."

"Not yet." Vivienne stepped away, her absence suddenly cold. "But you will. If you're brave enough."

She moved to the shelf, selected one of the violet bottles. "We don't empower women here, Zelda. Empowerment is a word that means nothing. We transform them. We teach them to weaponize what the world has told them to hide."

"How?"

Vivienne turned, the bottle cradled in her palms. "Through radical honesty. Through rituals that break conditioning. Through understanding that your body—aging, imperfect, real—is currency more valuable than youth. Because it comes with intelligence, with understanding, with the ability to read desire and exploit it."

The words hung in the air. Somewhere deeper in the loft, Zelda could hear voices—women chanting something in unison, the words indistinct but rhythmic, almost hypnotic.

"What is that?" she asked.

"A Radiance Session. Women learning to reprogram their shame into strength." Vivienne set the bottle on a small table beside the massage table. "Would you like to observe?"

Every instinct screamed at Zelda to leave. This wasn't a women's support group. This was something else, something that felt dangerous in ways she couldn't articulate.

But Vivienne was looking at her with those fathomless eyes, and Zelda heard herself say, "Yes."

"Good." Vivienne smiled, and this time it reached her eyes. "Then take off your blazer. You'll find our sessions work better when we're not hiding."

Zelda's hands moved to her buttons. The blazer slipped from her shoulders, and the air felt suddenly cooler against her bare arms in their sensible short-sleeved blouse.

"Better," Vivienne said softly. "The first step is always removing what protects you."

She pulled back another silk curtain.

### The First Mark

# THE PORCELAIN EMPRESS

## Chapter 3: The First Mark

The room beyond the curtain was smaller, more intimate. Six women sat in a circle on the floor, legs crossed, hands resting palm-up on their knees. They wore those same white robes, their faces illuminated by candles arranged in a precise geometric pattern around them. Their eyes were closed, heads tilted slightly back.

They were speaking in unison, but the words took Zelda a moment to parse:

*"I am not what they made me. I am what I claim."*

Over and over, the rhythm hypnotic, washing against her like waves.

Vivienne's hand pressed gently against the small of Zelda's back, guiding her to stand just outside the circle. "This is the Foundation Chant," she whispered. "The first truth we teach. That everything you've been told about your value—it's a lie designed to keep you small."

The women's voices rose and fell. Zelda found her own lips forming the words involuntarily: *I am not what they made me...*

"Watch," Vivienne said.

One of the women—a brunette in her mid-thirties with the remnants of a corporate haircut—opened her eyes. Her pupils were dilated, almost black in the candlelight. She rose smoothly, as if pulled by invisible strings, and moved to the center of the circle. The chanting continued around her.

Vivienne gestured, and another figure emerged from the shadows—a woman Zelda hadn't noticed before, younger, perhaps twenty-five, dressed all in black. She carried a small leather case.

"This is Iris," Vivienne said. "Our body artist. She helps women mark their transformation. Make it permanent."

The brunette in the center began removing her robe. Zelda's breath caught, instinct telling her to look away, but Vivienne's hand remained firm against her back. The woman's body was ordinary—neither beautiful nor ugly, just human. Soft belly, stretch marks silvering her hips, breasts that had fed children or simply lived forty years on earth.

She was smiling.

Iris opened her case. Inside, nestled in black velvet, were what looked like medical instruments. Fine needles. Small bottles of ink. The candlelight caught on steel.

"What is this?" Zelda heard herself ask.

"The First Mark," Vivienne said. "A tattoo, small and private, placed where only she—and those she chooses—will ever see it. A reminder that her body belongs to her now. Not to employers who ignore her. Not to a husband who left her. Not to children who've grown distant. To her."

The woman lay down on a cushioned mat that had appeared. Iris knelt beside her, cleaning an area just below her left hip with clinical efficiency. The other women continued their chant, the words forming a cocoon of sound.

"It will hurt," Vivienne said, still speaking low into Zelda's ear. "But she'll feel the pain differently now. Not as something being done to her. As something she's chosen. Do you understand the difference?"

Zelda did. And didn't. Her heart hammered against her ribs.

The needle touched skin. The woman gasped—not in pain, but something else. Something that made Zelda's stomach tighten in a way she couldn't name. Iris worked in silence, the needle moving in precise patterns, leaving behind dark lines that would never fade.

The chanting grew louder: *"I am not what they made me. I am what I claim."*

The woman on the mat began to weep. But she was still smiling.

After ten minutes that felt like hours, Iris sat back. The tattoo was complete—a small symbol, elegant and deliberate, just below the woman's hip bone. It looked like a keyhole. Or a portal. Or an eye.

"Perfect," Vivienne said. She moved into the circle, offering her hand to the marked woman. "Welcome to the next phase, Catherine."

Catherine—the brunette—took Vivienne's hand and rose. The other women broke their chant and embraced her, touching her bare skin without hesitation, their approval palpable and complete.

Zelda realized she was crying.

"It's overwhelming the first time you witness it," Vivienne said, turning back to her. "Seeing a woman claim her body as territory. Most of us have never seen that before. We've only seen bodies as problems to solve, weight to lose, aging to fight." She touched Zelda's cheek, catching a tear. "But you felt something just now, didn't you?"

"I don't know what I felt."

"Envy," Vivienne said simply. "You want what she has. That certainty. That ownership."

The word felt too large, too dangerous. Zelda shook her head, but Vivienne's hand moved to cup her chin, forcing eye contact.

"Don't lie. Not here. We don't have patience for the lies women tell themselves." Her thumb brushed across Zelda's lower lip. "You came here because you're invisible. Because you've spent forty years being good and appropriate and modest, and it's given you exactly nothing. You want to be seen. You want to matter. You want power."

"That's not—" But the words died. Because it was true. All of it.

"The First Mark is always offered, never required," Vivienne said. "But I think you need it. Something to remind you that this is real. That you were here, in this moment, when you decided to become someone new."

Iris was cleaning her instruments, packing them carefully back into their velvet case. The other women had helped Catherine back into her robe. They were watching Zelda now, their eyes knowing.

"Where?" Zelda heard herself ask.

Vivienne smiled. "Somewhere private. Somewhere only you will see in your ordinary life. But somewhere you can touch when you need to remember your choice."

Zelda's hand moved to her own hip, mirroring where Catherine's mark had been placed. Beneath her sensible skirt, beneath the control-top pantyhose she wore even though no one ever saw her legs. Skin that hadn't been touched by another person in three years.

"Just like that," Vivienne said softly. "Iris, prepare the station."

This was insane. Zelda should leave. Should run back to her studio apartment and her cheap wine and her careful, small existence. Should forget this place existed.

But Catherine was glowing now, radiant with something Zelda had never possessed, and the other women were smiling at her with welcome, and Vivienne's hand was warm and certain on her shoulder, and somewhere deep in her chest, a door she'd kept locked for forty years was beginning to open.

"I'll need you to remove your skirt and stockings," Iris said, her voice professional, medical. "You can keep your underwear on. Lie down here."

Zelda's hands found her zipper. The skirt pooled at her feet. The pantyhose whispered down her legs. She folded them carefully—instinct, always folding, always neat—and set them aside.

The mat was soft beneath her. The candles threw shadows across the ceiling. Vivienne knelt beside her, one cool hand resting on Zelda's stomach.

"Breathe," she said. "This is your choice. Your mark. Your body."

The needle touched her skin, and Zelda understood for the first time in her life what it meant to feel.

Not pleasant. Not entirely painful. Something electric and claiming and utterly present.

The women began to chant again: *"I am not what they made me. I am what I claim."*

And Zelda's lips moved with theirs.

### The Escalation

# THE PORCELAIN EMPRESS

## Chapter 4: The Escalation

The mark burned for three days.

Not infection—Iris had been meticulous about aftercare, providing ointment in a small amber jar that smelled of tea tree and something else Zelda couldn't identify. But beneath the physical healing, something deeper burned. Every time she sat at her desk, every time she crossed her legs or shifted in her chair, she felt it. A small constellation of ink just below her hip bone, hidden beneath her conservative skirts and pantyhose.

A secret that made her skin hum.

"Zelda, I need the Patterson files."

Amber's voice cut through her thoughts. The new Senior Executive Assistant stood beside Zelda's relegated desk—no longer outside Hendricks's office but tucked in the general administrative pool near the bathrooms. Amber held a latte in one manicured hand, her engagement ring catching the fluorescent light.

"Of course." Zelda reached for the filing cabinet, and the movement pulled at the tender skin. She bit back a gasp that wasn't entirely pain.

"Are you okay?" Amber's concern was performative, the kind offered because witnesses were present.

"Fine." Zelda handed over the files. "Anything else?"

"No, that's all." Amber turned to leave, then paused. "Oh, and Mr. Hendricks needs someone to work Saturday. I have my engagement party, so I can't. You don't mind, do you?"

It wasn't a question.

Zelda felt the old response forming—*Of course, no problem, happy to help*—but something stopped her. The mark burned. Vivienne's voice echoed in her memory: *You've spent forty years being good and appropriate and modest, and it's given you exactly nothing.*

"Actually," Zelda said, her voice steadier than she'd expected, "I have plans this Saturday."

Amber's perfectly shaped eyebrows rose. "Plans?"

"Yes. Plans." Zelda met her gaze directly. "Perhaps you could ask one of the junior assistants."

The silence stretched for three heartbeats. Then Amber's expression shifted—something between surprise and reassessment flickering across her face.

"Sure," she said slowly. "I'll ask someone else."

After she left, Zelda's hands trembled slightly. She touched her hip through her skirt, feeling the raised lines beneath layers of fabric. Her phone buzzed.

A text from an unknown number: *"Radiance Session tonight. 8 PM. Your presence is requested."*

She hadn't given them her number.

But she'd given them something more essential three nights ago, lying on that mat while Iris's needle claimed her skin. She'd given them permission to see her.

---

The third floor loft felt different this time. More women than before—perhaps twenty, arranged in small groups throughout the space. Some wore the white robes. Others dressed in street clothes like Zelda, their postures uncertain, new.

Vivienne emerged from behind the silk curtains wearing deep burgundy tonight, the color of old wine or fresh blood. Her hair was down, falling in dark waves past her shoulders. She looked younger. Dangerous.

"Zelda." Her smile was warm, genuine. "Come. I want you to meet some of our Inner Circle."

She led Zelda past the meditation spaces into a room Zelda hadn't seen before. Here, the aesthetic shifted. No candles, no silk curtains. Instead, sleek furniture, a full bar, modern art on exposed brick walls. Three women sat on a leather sectional, wine glasses in hand, their conversation pausing as Vivienne entered.

These women radiated something different from the robed Initiates. Confidence that bordered on predatory. The kind of beauty that came from knowing exactly what you were worth and how to leverage it.

"Ladies, this is Zelda Blackwood. She received her First Mark three days ago." Vivienne's hand rested on Zelda's lower back, proprietary. "Zelda, meet Simone, Diana, and Portia. They've been with Luminescence for two years."

Simone was Black, mid-forties, her natural hair styled in elaborate braids threaded with gold. She wore a silk blouse unbuttoned just low enough to be intentional. "Welcome to the real work," she said, her voice honeyed and sharp.

Diana looked like a former soccer mom—blonde, athletic, early forties. But there was nothing maternal about the way she assessed Zelda. "How does the mark feel?"

"It burns," Zelda admitted.

"Good." Portia was the oldest, perhaps fifty, with silver hair she wore cropped short and violet-framed glasses. "That means you're paying attention to it. Remembering it exists. That's the point."

Vivienne guided Zelda to sit. Poured her a glass of red wine from a bottle without a label. "These women are what you could become. What you will become, if you're willing to do the actual work of transformation."

"I thought the sessions were the work," Zelda said.

The four women exchanged glances. Simone laughed, low and knowing.

"The sessions are kindergarten," Diana said. "They teach you to stop apologizing for existing. But the real work?" She leaned forward. "That's learning to monetize what you've finally claimed."

Zelda's stomach tightened. "Monetize?"

"Your body. Your presence. Your understanding of what men want." Portia sipped her wine. "You're forty. You think that's a liability. But there's a market for women exactly like you. Successful men who are bored with escorts who look like Amber. Who want someone who can hold a conversation. Who understands discretion. Who knows how to make them feel powerful while actually controlling every moment of the encounter."

The words landed like stones in still water, ripples spreading outward.

"I'm not a—" Zelda started.

"A prostitute?" Vivienne's voice was gentle but firm. "No. You're not. You're a woman learning that sex work and corporate work aren't that different. Both require you to perform. To manage egos. To make men feel important while they take what they want." She touched Zelda's cheek. "The only difference is sex workers get paid what they're actually worth."

"This is what Luminescence really does?" Zelda's voice came out smaller than she intended.

"Among other things." Simone stood, moved to the bar to refill her glass. "We have women who do traditional escort work. Others who serve as 'consultants' to wealthy men—companions for business dinners, weekend trips. Some specialize in domination. Some in the girlfriend experience." She turned back, her smile sharp. "We're very entrepreneurial."

"And we only accept women who have something special," Diana added. "Women with enough life experience to understand the game. Vivienne is very selective."

Zelda should leave. Should run. Should report this place to someone, though she wasn't sure who or for what. But the mark on her hip burned, and these women looked at her with something that felt like respect, and somewhere beneath her shock, a small voice whispered: *How much money?*

Vivienne seemed to hear the unspoken question. "Simone cleared six thousand dollars last week. Four clients. Portia specializes in long-term arrangements—she has two men who pay her five thousand monthly each for availability."

"Ten thousand dollars a month?" Zelda heard herself ask.

"For perhaps twenty hours of actual work," Portia confirmed. "Less than you spend making coffee for men who don't remember your name."

The wine was making Zelda's head fuzzy. Or maybe it wasn't the wine. Maybe it was the numbers, the possibility, the way these women looked at her like she could be one of them.

"I've never..." she started.

"None of us had," Diana said softly. "That's not the point. The point is: are you tired of being invisible? Are you ready to learn what real power feels like?"

Behind them, a door opened. A man entered—fifties, expensive suit, silver hair. He didn't seem surprised to find them there. Simone rose smoothly, all predatory grace.

"Gentlemen aren't usually allowed in the loft," Vivienne explained. "But Gerald is a benefactor. He helps fund our operations. Simone has been seeing him for six months."

Gerald crossed to Simone, kissed her cheek with clear affection. Pulled an envelope from his jacket and handed it to her. She accepted it with a smile that looked genuine, tucking it into her clutch without checking the contents.

"Thank you, darling," she purred. "Same time next week?"

"Wouldn't miss it." He nodded to Vivienne, ignored the rest of them, and left.

Simone opened the envelope. Hundred-dollar bills, at least thirty of them. She fanned them casually, then caught Zelda's stunned expression.

"He's sweet," she said. "Lonely. Divorced. Needs someone to talk to as much as anything else. Three hours of my time. Three thousand dollars. And he thinks I'm doing him a favor."

Zelda's secretary salary was thirty-eight thousand annually. Before taxes.

"We're not asking you to decide tonight," Vivienne said. "But you need to understand what Luminescence really offers. Not empowerment. Not sisterhood. Those are just words." She moved behind Zelda's chair, her hands coming to rest on Zelda's shoulders. "We offer transformation into women who take what they want from a world that's taken everything from them."

Her fingers found the top button of Zelda's blouse. "The next step requires more trust. More vulnerability. Are you ready?"

Every instinct screamed no. But Zelda touched her hip, felt the mark burning beneath her skirt, and heard herself whisper: "Yes."

Vivienne's smile reflected in the windows overlooking the dark city below. "Then let's begin the real education."

### The Cognitive Shift

# THE PORCELAIN EMPRESS

## Chapter 5: The Cognitive Shift

Vivienne's fingers paused at the second button of Zelda's blouse. Not opening it. Just resting there, a question made physical.

"Before we continue," she said, her voice dropping to that intimate register that seemed to bypass Zelda's ears and speak directly to her nervous system, "I need you to understand something fundamental. The next phase isn't about sex. Not really. It's about control."

Diana rose from the sectional, moved to stand beside Vivienne. Together, they created a wall of feminine certainty that made Zelda feel simultaneously protected and trapped.

"Most women spend their lives being controlled," Diana said. "By fathers, boyfriends, husbands, bosses. We're taught to manage men's emotions, anticipate their needs, make ourselves smaller so they can feel bigger." She crouched down to Zelda's eye level. "What we teach here is the opposite. How to make men smaller. How to make them need us so desperately that they'll pay anything, do anything, just to exist in our presence for an hour."

"That sounds manipulative," Zelda heard herself say.

Portia laughed from the sectional. "That's exactly what it is. And it's exactly what's been done to you your entire life. We're just teaching you to wield the knife instead of offering your throat to it."

Zelda's mind felt strange—fractured, like she was observing herself from a distance. Part of her wanted to stand, to leave, to return to her small apartment and pretend this place didn't exist. But that part was getting quieter, drowned out by something else. Something that had been growing since Iris's needle first touched her skin.

Curiosity. Hunger. A dark flower unfurling in her chest.

"I don't know how to control anyone," she said.

"Yes, you do." Vivienne's fingers finally moved, slipping the button free. The blouse gaped slightly, revealing the practical beige bra beneath. "You've been doing it for twenty-two years. Managing Hendricks's moods, anticipating his needs, making yourself indispensable while pretending to be invisible. That's a kind of control. We're just going to teach you to be conscious of it. To use it intentionally."

The second button opened. Then the third. Zelda's breath came faster, but she didn't stop Vivienne's hands. Didn't say no. The word existed somewhere in her vocabulary, but it felt very far away.

"The male ego is remarkably simple," Simone said from across the room. She'd poured herself another glass of wine and was watching Zelda with clinical interest. "They want to feel powerful while actually being taken care of. They want to believe they're in control while you orchestrate every moment. It's exhausting when you do it for free in an office. It's intoxicating when you do it for three thousand dollars and they thank you afterward."

The blouse hung open now, revealing Zelda's modest bra, her soft stomach, the landscape of a body she'd spent forty years trying to hide or fix or apologize for. She should feel exposed. Humiliated. But Vivienne and Diana were looking at her with something like appreciation, and that small dark flower in her chest bloomed wider.

"Stand up," Vivienne said.

Zelda stood. Her legs felt uncertain, but she stood.

"Let the blouse fall."

She did. It pooled at her feet, and she stood in her skirt and bra and pantyhose, her practical pumps, all the armor of invisibility that had never actually protected her from anything.

"You're forty years old," Diana said, circling her slowly. "You have stretch marks. Cellulite. Your breasts aren't what they were at twenty-five. Your stomach is soft. And every single one of those things is valuable. Do you know why?"

Zelda shook her head.

"Because you've lived," Portia said from the sectional. "And men who are worth the money we charge—they've lived too. They're bored of perfect twenty-five-year-old bodies that come with perfect twenty-five-year-old neediness. They want women who won't fall in love. Who won't make demands. Who understand that the arrangement is transactional and can navigate it with intelligence and grace."

Diana's hand traced the waistband of Zelda's skirt. "They want women who look like you. Real. Mature. Capable of making them feel like gods while actually treating them like children who need to be managed."

Something was happening in Zelda's mind—a shifting, like tectonic plates grinding against each other. The logic that had governed her life for forty years (be good, be modest, work hard, wait for your reward) was fracturing, and through the cracks, something else was seeping in.

What if being good had been the trap all along? What if modesty was just another word for making men comfortable with your diminishment? What if the reward for waiting was nothing but more waiting?

"Touch yourself," Vivienne said suddenly.

Zelda blinked. "What?"
