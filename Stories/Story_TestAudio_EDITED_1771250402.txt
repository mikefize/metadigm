# Cult: The Wellness Doll

### Chapter 1: The Weight of Hands

The arterial line monitor screamed its single note. Karen's hands moved before her brain could catch up—two fingers to the carotid, other hand slamming the code button. Mr. Patterson's chest was still warm under her palm.

"Code Blue, ICU bed seven. Code Blue, ICU bed seven."

She started compressions. Counted. The cartilage in his sternum gave that distinctive crack under her locked elbows. Thirty compressions. Two breaths. The Ambu bag hissed.

"Where the fuck is everyone?" she said to the empty room.

Thirty more. Two more. The monitor flatlined with perfect indifference.

Dr. Reeves arrived six minutes later, his coffee still steaming. By then, Karen's arms were burning and Mr. Patterson's lips had gone from blue to grey. The code team went through the motions—epi, atropine, the paddles—but they all knew. She'd been covering four patients in a unit built for one-to-one care. She'd been checking Mr. Patterson's IV antibiotics when his pressure dropped in bed seven.

Four patients. One nurse. Thirty-seven minutes from decompensation to death.

"Time of death, 6:47 AM," Dr. Reeves said, not meeting her eyes.

Karen pulled off her gloves. Her hands were shaking. Someone else would clean the body. Someone else would call the family. She had three other patients who needed medications and wound care and someone to notice if their organs started failing.

The rest of her shift blurred into a sequence of tasks her body completed while her mind replayed the monitor's alarm. Again. Again. The sound had become part of her pulse.

Her apartment smelled like old takeout and medical textbooks. Karen dropped her bag by the door and stood in the kitchen, staring at the stack of envelopes on the counter. She didn't need to open them. She knew what they said.

*Great Lakes Student Loans: Minimum Payment Due $891.00*
*Federal Direct Unsubsidized: Current Balance $84,337.16*

She'd done the math once, late at night with a bottle of wine and a calculator. At her current salary, after taxes and minimum payments and rent, she had $340 a month for everything else. Food. Gas. The cost of existing.

Four years of nursing school. One hundred and twenty-seven thousand dollars in total loans. Thirty-seven minutes of a man dying because she was spread too thin.

Her phone buzzed. She ignored it. It buzzed again.

*Mira: You okay? Saw what happened with Patterson.*
*Mira: Want to get drinks?*

Karen stared at the texts. Mira worked in the ED—she'd seen worse, lost more. Somehow she still showed up every shift with her lipstick perfect and her laugh loud enough to cut through the chaos.

*Karen: Can't. Too tired.*
*Mira: Bullshit. You're spiraling. I'm picking you up at 8.*

The bar was the kind of place that pretended to be upscale with dim lighting and overpriced cocktails. Mira ordered them both something with elderflower. Karen watched the bartender muddle herbs and thought about Mr. Patterson's dilated pupils, the way his jaw had gone slack when the third round of epi did nothing.

"Stop," Mira said.

"Stop what?"

"You're doing the thing. The replay thing." Mira pushed the drink toward her. "It wasn't your fault."

"I was supposed to—"

"You were supposed to have a safe patient ratio. You were supposed to have adequate staffing. You were supposed to work in a system that didn't treat nurses like disposable labor." Mira took a long drink. "But sure, blame yourself. That's easier than blaming the hospital that cleared two million in profit last quarter while scheduling one nurse for four ICU patients."

Karen's throat felt tight. "He had grandkids. His daughter showed me pictures last week."

"I know." Mira's hand covered hers. "I know it feels like it's on you. But baby, you're drowning. And they're still going to schedule you the same way tomorrow."

The elderflower tasted like guilt. Karen drank it anyway.

"I have something," Mira said. "Something that helped me when I was where you are."

"I'm fine."

"You're a hundred and twenty-seven thousand dollars in debt, working yourself into an early grave for a hospital that would replace you before your body went cold. You haven't slept more than four hours in weeks. When's the last time you ate something that wasn't from a vending machine?"

Karen didn't answer.

Mira pulled out her phone, scrolled, and turned the screen toward her. The website was elegant—white space and serif fonts and women in white linen looking peaceful. *Luminari*. The word itself felt expensive.

"It's a wellness collective," Mira said. "Women only. They focus on healing burnout and financial trauma. I started going three months ago."

"I can't afford—"

"First session is free. And trust me, once you understand their philosophy, the money thing... it shifts." Mira's smile was different than usual. Calmer. Like she'd discovered something that made the rest of the world make sense. "They taught me that my body isn't just a tool for serving other people. It's an asset. My highest-value asset, actually."

Karen looked at the website. The women in the photos had the kind of bone structure that suggested they'd never worried about student loans. "I don't know."

"One session. Tomorrow's your day off, right? Just come with me. Worst case, you spend two hours doing meditation with some rich ladies. Best case..." Mira's eyes were bright. "Best case, you remember that you're worth more than what the hospital is paying you."

The ice in Karen's drink melted. She thought about going home to her empty apartment, to the envelopes on the counter, to the sound of Mr. Patterson's monitor screaming in her memory. She thought about showing up for another shift where four patients would need her and she'd have to choose who got her attention and pray no one died in the minutes she wasn't watching.

"Okay," she said. "One session."

Mira squeezed her hand. "You're going to love Sienna. She was a nurse too. ICU, actually. She gets it."

Something in Mira's voice made Karen's skin prickle, but exhaustion had worn her instincts down to nothing. She finished her drink and let Mira order another round. Outside, the city hummed with people who weren't drowning. Karen wondered what that felt like.

### Chapter 2: The Threshold

The Luminari building didn't look like a cult. It looked like money.

Karen stood on the sidewalk, staring up at the converted brownstone in Lincoln Park. Three stories of blonde brick and French windows, the kind of place where lake views added another zero to the rent. A discreet bronze plaque by the door read *Luminari Collective: Wellness Sanctuary for Women*. No address. If you didn't know where it was, you didn't belong.

Her scrubs from yesterday were still in her car. She'd thrown on jeans and the one sweater that didn't have coffee stains, but standing here, she felt like a smudge on expensive paper.

Mira squeezed her arm. "Stop overthinking. You belong here just as much as anyone."

The door opened before they could knock. The woman who answered had the kind of beauty that came from discipline—sharp collarbone, hair in a perfect low bun, skin that suggested she'd never worked a night shift in her life. She wore head-to-toe ivory: silk wrap top, linen pants, bare feet with a pedicure that probably cost more than Karen's car payment.

"Mira, gorgeous." They kissed both cheeks. "And you must be Karen. I'm Sienna."

Her handshake was warm and firm. Nurse hands, Karen thought automatically. You could always tell—strong from years of lifting patients, soft from constant washing. But Sienna's nails were immaculate, nude gel with perfect cuticles. No one who worked bedside kept nails like that.

"Come in, come in. We're just finishing morning gratitude."

The interior smelled like white sage and something sweeter—jasmine, maybe, or vanilla. The floors were original hardwood, restored to a pale gleam. Everything was white or cream or the palest grey. A crystal chandelier caught the morning light and scattered it across the walls like a promise.

Voices drifted from the back of the house. Female voices in rhythmic harmony, chanting something Karen couldn't quite make out.

"We start every day at four AM," Sienna said, leading them down a hallway lined with black-and-white photographs. Women laughing, women in meditation, women holding champagne flutes on rooftops. "Gratitude circles. It's about claiming your energy before the world tries to take it."

Four AM. Karen's stomach tightened. "That's... early."

"It's sacred." Sienna glanced back, and her smile was understanding, almost pitying. "I know how it sounds. I used to think I needed eight hours too. But that's scarcity thinking. Sleep is just another way we've been taught to be small."

They entered a large room that must have been a dining room once. Now it was empty except for white cushions arranged in a circle on the floor. Fifteen women sat cross-legged, eyes closed, swaying slightly. They wore variations of the same uniform—expensive athleisure in muted tones, no jewelry except delicate gold chains, faces bare or barely touched with makeup.

"I am worthy of abundance," they chanted in unison. "My body is my temple. My worth is infinite."

Karen felt Mira settle onto a cushion, tugging her down too. Sienna took her place at the head of the circle, and the chanting shifted, grew softer.

"My highest-value asset," Sienna said, and the others echoed her. "My sacred currency."

Karen's skin prickled. She'd expected generic wellness bullshit—manifestation, chakras, maybe some borderline-legal diet advice. But this felt choreographed, rehearsed. The women's voices layered over each other with practiced precision.

"Let's welcome our new sister," Sienna said, and suddenly fifteen pairs of eyes were on Karen. They were all smiling. Warm, genuine smiles that should have been comforting but instead felt hungry. "Karen, would you like to share what brought you here today?"

Her mouth was dry. "I... Mira invited me."

"What do you do, Karen?"

"I'm a nurse. ICU."

Something rippled through the circle. A few women nodded. One whispered to her neighbor.

"A healer," Sienna said softly. "Of course. Tell me, Karen—when was the last time someone healed you?"

The question hit harder than it should have. Karen thought about Mr. Patterson, about the compression cracks under her palms, about coming home to student loan statements and falling asleep still smelling like hospital antiseptic. When had anyone—

"That hesitation tells me everything." Sienna leaned forward. Her top gaped slightly, and Karen caught a glimpse of black lace underneath the silk. Expensive lingerie. The kind that cost more than Karen made in a day. "You've been trained to give yourself away. Your time, your energy, your body—you pour it all into other people. For what? Sixty thousand a year? Before taxes?"

Fifty-eight, actually, but Karen didn't correct her.

"How much do you owe in student loans?"

The question should have felt invasive. Instead, it felt like permission. "A hundred and twenty-seven thousand."

Several women gasped. One put her hand over her heart. "Jesus," someone whispered.

"Four years of school," Sienna said. "Four years of paying for the privilege of learning how to sacrifice your body for others. And now you're trapped. They own you. The hospitals, the loan companies—they've convinced you that your purpose is to bleed yourself dry for patients who won't remember your name and administrators who see you as a line item."

Karen's throat burned. She wanted to argue, but the words wouldn't come.

"What if I told you," Sienna continued, her voice dropping to something intimate, "that your body is worth more than you can possibly imagine? That those same hands you use to save lives could earn in one night what you currently make in a month?"

The room went very still.

"That's the lie they sold you with that nursing degree. That caring for others is noble, that sacrifice is virtue. But nobility doesn't pay off debt. Virtue doesn't buy you freedom." Sienna's smile was gentle, understanding. "Your body is an asset, Karen. The highest-value asset you'll ever own. And right now, you're practically giving it away."

Karen's pulse hammered in her ears. This wasn't wellness advice. This was something else entirely.

"I don't—" she started.

"You don't have to decide anything today," Sienna said. "Just listen. Learn. Let us show you what's possible when you stop apologizing for your worth." She stood, fluid and graceful. "Now. Let's talk about the difference between giving and receiving."

The other women rose as one. Mira touched Karen's elbow, guiding her up. And as they filed into the next room—another space of cream and crystal and expensive emptiness—Karen noticed something she'd missed before. Every woman was wearing lingerie under her clothes. Small glimpses of lace and silk where fabric shifted, where tops gaped, where pants sat low on hips. Not workout clothes. Not comfort. Presentation.

The door closed behind them with a soft, final click.

### Chapter 3: The First Mark

The second room had mirrors. Floor-to-ceiling on two walls, reflecting everything back at itself until the space felt infinite. Karen saw herself multiplied—fifteen versions of her uncomfortable posture, her Target sweater, her exhaustion. The other women looked like they belonged in the reflections. She looked like a mistake.

"Presentation," Sienna said, moving to the center of the room, "is the first language of value. Before you speak, before you touch, before you offer any service—your body communicates worth."

She turned slowly, and Karen watched her reflection do the same. The silk of Sienna's wrap top caught light like water. Underneath, the black lace was visible now, deliberate. A peek of something expensive, something chosen.

"In nursing, they taught you to disappear. Scrubs in shapeless colors. Hair back. Nails short. No perfume, no jewelry, nothing that says 'I am a woman with a body that has value beyond its function.'" Sienna's eyes found Karen in the mirror. "They trained you to be invisible. Undesirable. Safe."

Karen's arms crossed over her chest automatically.

"That instinct right there." Sienna gestured. "Hiding yourself. Making yourself smaller. That's conditioning. And it's costing you everything."

One of the women—late twenties, blonde, perfect minimal makeup—stepped forward. "I was a teacher," she said. "Forty-two thousand a year. Student loans, credit card debt from buying classroom supplies my school wouldn't cover. I was drowning." She smiled. "Last month I made sixty thousand dollars."

The number hit Karen's chest like a physical blow. Sixty thousand. In one month.

"How?" Karen's voice cracked.

"By understanding that men will pay extraordinary amounts for the fantasy of access," the woman said simply. "Not even sex. Just proximity. The possibility of it."

Sienna nodded. "This is what they don't want you to know. Your body—especially your youth, your femininity, your perceived innocence—is the most valuable commodity in the world. More than your nursing degree. More than any skill you learned in school. And you can monetize it without ever compromising your boundaries."

She moved to a cabinet Karen hadn't noticed and pulled out a black box. Inside, tissue paper rustled.

"Sugar dating," Sienna said. "It's not what you think. It's not prostitution. It's companionship. Dinner dates. Shopping trips. Sometimes just conversation. Men pay for your time, your attention, your presence. They pay for the way you make them feel."

She lifted out lingerie—a set in deep emerald green, all lace and strategic cutouts. La Perla, Karen recognized the label from a Nordstrom window once. Probably four hundred dollars.

"This is an investment," Sienna said. "When you meet a potential arrangement, you're selling a fantasy. The fantasy requires the right presentation. Confidence. Intentional sensuality. The promise of something rare."

Karen's mouth was dry. "I can't—"

"You can't afford not to." Sienna's voice was gentle but firm. "How many more Mr. Pattersons can you survive? How many more years of giving yourself away for pennies while your debt compounds?"

The name hit like a slap. Karen's hands clenched. How did she—

"Mira told me," Sienna said, reading her face. "About yesterday. About the code. Four patients, one nurse. You were set up to fail, Karen. And when that man died, did the hospital take responsibility? Or did they let you carry it?"

Karen's eyes burned.

"That guilt you're feeling? They weaponize it. They use your empathy, your sense of duty, to keep you compliant. Overworked. Underpaid. Grateful just to have a job." Sienna set the lingerie on a nearby bench. "What if you could pay off your loans in a year? What if you never had to choose between patients again because you didn't need the hospital?"

Around the circle, women nodded. Several had tears in their eyes, like they were remembering their own breaking points.

"I'm not—" Karen's voice failed. "I can't just—"

"You're not doing anything today," Sienna said. "Today you're just going to understand the economics. How much you're actually worth."

She pulled out her phone and opened an app. *SeekingArrangement*, the interface read. Profiles of women—beautiful women, but not model-beautiful. Attainable. Girl-next-door. Several looked like they could be nurses, teachers, or graduate students.

"Here." Sienna scrolled. "Twenty-five, Chicago, college-educated, fit. Standard monthly arrangement: five to ten thousand dollars. For what? Eight to twelve hours a month. Dinners. Maybe a weekend trip. Conversation. The girlfriend experience without the commitment."

She turned the phone toward Karen. The numbers were real. Reviews from men, testimonials, verification badges.

"Some want more," Sienna continued. "Physical intimacy. But that's always your choice. Your boundary. And the compensation reflects it. Ten thousand. Twenty. I've seen fifty for the right arrangement with the right client."

Karen's head felt light. Fifty thousand dollars. For...

"You're already selling your body," Sienna said softly. "Right now, you're selling it to a hospital. Twelve-hour shifts, lifting patients who weigh twice what you do, exposure to diseases, violence from patients and families. Your back will give out in ten years. Your empathy is already dying. And for what? Sixty thousand a year. Four thousand dollars of that goes to loan interest alone. You're paying them for the privilege of destroying yourself."

The math was too easy. Karen had done it herself, late at night, staring at her statements. At her current rate, she'd be paying off loans until she was fifty. If her body lasted that long. If the burnout didn't kill her first.

"I have someone," Sienna said. "A gentleman I've known for three years. Extremely generous, very safe. He's looking for a new arrangement—his previous sugar baby just paid off her medical school loans and moved to residency." She smiled. "He has a thing for nurses, actually. Finds the caretaking instinct arousing."

Karen's skin felt too hot.

"One dinner," Sienna said. "Public place, no expectations beyond conversation. He pays two thousand just for the first meeting. Two thousand dollars, Karen. For two hours of your time, wearing a nice dress, being charming over good wine."

Two thousand dollars. Karen's rent. Her car payment. Her minimum loan payment. All in one night.

"If you feel uncomfortable, you leave. No harm, no obligation." Sienna's hand was warm on Karen's shoulder. "But I think you'll find it's easier than you imagine. You already know how to perform emotional labor. You do it every shift. This is just... better compensated."

The room was too quiet. Karen could hear her own heartbeat, feel the weight of fifteen gazes on her skin.

"Just think about it," Sienna said. "No pressure. Come to gratitude circle tomorrow morning. Four AM. Meet the other sisters. Hear their stories. Then decide."

Four AM. The same time Karen usually woke for shift prep. The same exhausted hours she gave to the hospital. What if she gave them somewhere else?

Mira squeezed her hand. "It changed my life," she whispered. "I paid off twelve thousand in credit cards in two months. I'm free, Kar. For the first time since nursing school, I'm actually free."

Karen looked at herself in the mirror. Multiplied. Infinite versions of a woman who'd been taught her worth was in her sacrifice. What if they were wrong?

"Okay," she heard herself say. "One dinner."

Sienna's smile was radiant. "Perfect. I'll make the introduction. His name is Richard. You're going to love him."

The lingerie in its tissue paper waited on the bench. Emerald green. La Perla. An investment. Karen's hands were shaking. The same hands that had cracked Mr. Patterson's sternum. The same hands that charted medications and hung IVs and tried to make sixty thousand dollars feel like enough.

Maybe they'd been worth more all along.

### Chapter 4: The Preparation

Karen didn't sleep. She lay in her bed watching the ceiling fan rotate, counting the hours until 4 AM. At 3:15, she gave up and showered. The water was scalding but she couldn't feel it. Her mind kept looping back to the same number: two thousand dollars.

Two hours of conversation. One dress. Two thousand dollars.

She'd worked a double last month—twenty-four hours straight, no breaks, one meal from the vending machine—and made four hundred and eighty dollars before taxes. A patient had thrown a bedpan at her face. Another had coded twice. She'd held a woman's hand while she died because there was no family, no one else, just Karen and the monitor's flatline and the smell of decay.

Four hundred and eighty dollars.

The math didn't work. Had never worked. She'd been telling herself it was noble, that sacrifice meant something, that her purpose was worth more than money. But nobility didn't call the loan companies. Purpose didn't keep the lights on.

At 3:50 AM, she was in her car. The city was different at this hour—empty streets, traffic lights blinking yellow, everything suspended between night and morning. She felt suspended too. Between the person she'd been yesterday and whoever she was becoming.

The Luminari building glowed from within. Through the French windows, she could see movement. Women in white, swaying in unison. Mira met her at the door, already dressed in ivory athleisure that probably cost more than Karen's entire wardrobe. Her face was bare, glowing. She looked younger somehow. Lighter.

"You came," Mira said, and pulled her into a hug that lasted too long.

"I couldn't sleep."

"Good. Sleep is overrated anyway." Mira's laugh was breathy, almost giddy. "You're going to feel so different after circle. The chanting, the energy—it's like shedding skin."

The gratitude circle was already in session. Karen slipped onto a cushion, Mira on one side, a woman she didn't recognize on the other. Sienna sat at the head, eyes closed, leading the chant.

"I release scarcity. I claim abundance. My body is wealth."

The voices layered, hypnotic. Karen mouthed the words at first, self-conscious. But the repetition had gravity. After the tenth cycle, she was speaking. After the twentieth, she wasn't thinking anymore.

"My worth is infinite. My value is inherent. I am the commodity."

The woman beside her swayed closer. Their shoulders touched. The warmth was startling, intimate. Karen found herself swaying too, matching the rhythm, her voice blending into the collective sound.

"I am open to receive. I am worthy of receiving. My body is my temple and my treasure."

Time dissolved. Could have been twenty minutes. Could have been two hours. Karen's thoughts smoothed out, simplified. The guilt about Mr. Patterson receded. The shame about considering Sienna's offer thinned. What remained was stark and clarifying: she needed money, she had a body, men would pay. Everything else was noise.

When the chanting stopped, the silence felt violent. Karen blinked, disoriented. The room was brighter. Her throat was raw.

"Beautiful," Sienna said. Her smile was proud, maternal. "I felt everyone's energy rise. Especially yours, Karen."

Karen's face flushed with something that felt uncomfortably close to pleasure.

"Now. Let's talk about Richard." Sienna produced a leather folder, the expensive kind that closed with a magnetic clasp. Inside were photos, printed on heavy cardstock. "Fifty-six. Venture capitalist. Divorced, no children. He has a penthouse in Gold Coast and a home in Aspen. Very generous, very discreet."

The photos showed a man who looked exactly like money—silver hair, expensive suit, the kind of tan that came from tropical vacations. Handsome in the way powerful men were handsome: groomed, confident, well-maintained.

"He prefers intelligence," Sienna continued. "Women with real careers, real substance. The nursing thing will intrigue him. He likes the caretaking dynamic."

Karen's stomach twisted. "What does that mean?"

"It means he wants to feel taken care of. Listened to. Attended to." Sienna's tone was clinical, instructive. "Men like Richard are surrounded by people who want something from them. You'll be the person who simply appreciates him. Who makes him feel seen."

"That's it?"

"For the first meeting, yes. Dinner at Gibson's. He'll ask about your work, your life. You'll be charming, interested, present. At the end of the night, he'll give you an envelope. Two thousand dollars, cash. If there's chemistry, you discuss the arrangement."

Sienna slid another photo across. A contract, professionally formatted. *Terms of Arrangement*, it read at the top. Below: financial compensation, time commitment, boundaries and expectations, termination clause.

"Ten thousand a month," Sienna said. "For approximately forty hours of your time. Dinners, events, maybe a weekend trip. He'll want intimacy eventually—most do—but that's negotiated separately. Another five to ten thousand, depending on frequency."

Karen's hands were shaking. Twenty thousand dollars a month. That was more than she made in four months at the hospital.

"I can't—" Her voice cracked. "I don't know how to—"

"You already know." Sienna's hand covered hers. "You've been performing emotional labor your entire adult life. Comforting patients. Managing doctors' egos. Soothing families. This is the same skill, just properly compensated."

Around the circle, women nodded. Several were smiling at her with something that looked like encouragement but felt like hunger.

"The problem," Sienna continued, "is that you've been taught to see your body as separate from your worth. Nursing school drilled that in. Your body is a tool for service, nothing more. But that's a lie designed to keep you controllable." She leaned closer. "Your body is the source of your power. Your youth, your beauty, your fertility—even if you never use it. Men will pay extraordinary amounts just to be near it."

Karen thought about Mr. Patterson's daughter, showing her photos of grandkids. Thought about the hospital's email last week announcing record profits while denying their union's request for better ratios. Thought about her apartment, her car, the envelopes on her counter that never stopped coming.

"When?" she heard herself ask.

Sienna's smile was triumphant. "Tomorrow night. I'll have a dress sent over. La Perla underneath—the emerald set from yesterday. It'll make you feel different. Powerful."

"I don't know my size in—"

"I do." Sienna gestured to one of the other women, who stepped forward with a measuring tape. "We take care of our sisters. Every detail."

The woman's hands were efficient, professional. Around bust, under bust, waist, hips. She called out numbers and someone else wrote them down. Karen stood there, being quantified, measured like meat. Like merchandise. Like an asset.

"Perfect," Sienna said when they finished. "You're going to be stunning. Richard won't be able to believe his luck."

The chanting started again. Different words this time, more specific.

"I am open to abundance. I receive without shame. My body is my wealth and my weapon."

Karen's lips moved automatically. The repetition was soothing, numbing. It erased the part of her brain that wanted to question, to analyze, to remember who she'd been seventy-two hours ago. That Karen was drowning. That Karen watched patients die because there weren't enough hands. That Karen was so fucking tired she couldn't remember what it felt like to not be tired.

This Karen was going to make two thousand dollars for eating dinner.

"My pleasure is my power," the circle chanted. "My desire is my currency."

Mira's hand found hers, squeezed. On her other side, the nameless woman did the same. The circle was connected, unified. Karen's voice rose with theirs.

"I choose my worth. I claim my value. I am the commodity and the consumer."

When it ended, Karen's throat was raw and her mind was quiet. Sienna announced "sister sessions" would continue until midnight—workshops on conversation skills, styling, and "energetic presentation."

Karen should have been exhausted. Should have been horrified. Instead, she felt something close to relief. She didn't have to be noble anymore. Didn't have to sacrifice. Didn't have to pour herself into a system that would grind her down to nothing and call it virtue. She could just take the fucking money.

"Stay," Mira whispered. "We're doing lingerie education next. You need to understand the psychology of presentation."

Karen stayed. Watched as Sienna and two other women modeled different styles—romantic versus provocative, innocent versus experienced. Learned that the right underwear was invisible armor, that men responded to specific colors and cuts, that her body was a language she'd never been taught to speak.

"This," Sienna said, holding up a sheer bodysuit, "communicates availability without desperation. Confidence without aggression. It says 'I know what I'm worth and I'm allowing you proximity.'"

She passed it to Karen. The fabric was so fine it felt like water.

"Try it on," Sienna said. "You need to understand how it feels. How it changes your posture, your presence."

The bathroom was marble and gold fixtures. Karen locked the door and stared at herself in the mirror. She looked like hell—dark circles, hospital pallor, the permanent tension in her jaw from clenching through shifts. She stripped. The bodysuit slid on like a second skin. In the mirror, she was transformed. Not beautiful, exactly, but deliberate. Intentional. Her body wasn't just a vehicle anymore—it was the product.

She thought about Mr. Patterson. About the crack of his sternum. About four patients and one nurse and thirty-seven minutes. Then she stopped thinking.

When she emerged, the women applauded. Sienna's eyes glittered with something that looked like pride but smelled like possession.

"Perfect," Sienna said. "You're ready."

Karen looked at herself in the floor-length mirrors. Fifteen versions of a woman in expensive lingerie, learning to monetize her flesh. She didn't recognize herself. She didn't want to.

### Chapter 5: The Transaction

The dress arrived at 3 PM in a black box with tissue paper that rustled like money. No return address. Karen opened it on her kitchen counter, next to the stack of unopened loan statements she'd stopped looking at.

Burgundy silk. Cowl neckline. The kind of dress that cost more than her weekly grocery budget. She held it up and the fabric moved like liquid, catching light, promising transformation. The emerald La Perla set was underneath. She'd worn it for an hour yesterday during "presentation practice"—Sienna teaching her how to walk, how to sit, how to cross her legs so the hem would ride up just enough. How to make her body into an invitation.

Karen should shower. Should eat something. Should sleep—she'd been awake for thirty-six hours between her last shift and this morning's gratitude circle and the afternoon "sister session" on conversation techniques. But sleep felt dangerous now, like it might let doubt creep back in.

Her phone buzzed. Sienna.

*Car service at 6:30. Gibson's Bar, 7 PM. Richard will be at the table by the window. Order whatever you want—he's covering everything. Smile. Listen. Let him talk about himself. The envelope will be on the table when you leave.*

Karen stared at the message. Two hours. In two hours, she'd be sitting across from a fifty-six-year-old venture capitalist, wearing lingerie that cost more than she made in a day, learning to monetize the same body she'd been taught to make invisible.

Her nursing scrubs were in a heap by the washing machine. She could see the ID badge clipped to the pocket—her photo from four years ago, fresh out of school, still believing the shit they'd told her about purpose and calling and service.

That girl was a fucking idiot.

The thought came so clearly it startled her. But it was true, wasn't it? Four years of debt for a job that paid barely enough to service the interest. Four years of learning to sacrifice her body, her time, her mental health for patients who wouldn't remember her name and a system that would replace her before her corpse was cold. She'd been so goddamn noble. So committed to caring, to helping, to pouring herself into strangers while her own life fell apart.

What the fuck had it gotten her? A dead patient. A mountain of debt. An apartment that smelled like depression and microwaved meals. The constant exhaustion of being not quite enough for too many people.

Meanwhile, Mira—who'd been just as broke three months ago—had paid off twelve thousand in credit cards. Was talking about a vacation to Bali. Had shown up to their last shift in Lululemon that still had the tags on, then laughed and said a "friend" had taken her shopping. A friend. Right.
