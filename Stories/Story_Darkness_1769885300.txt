### The Status Quo

# The Silver Case

The fluorescent lights of the *Herald Tribune* newsroom hummed their usual evening dirge as Eunice Mercer leaned back in her ergonomic chair, fingers still trembling slightly from the adrenaline of publication. Her exposé on Meridian Pharmaceuticals had gone live three hours ago, and the internet was already ablaze.

*CEO LUCAS VANE INDICTED: Internal Documents Reveal Deadly Clinical Trial Cover-Up*

The headline was hers. The byline was hers. The satisfaction should have been hers too, but instead she felt only the familiar hollowness that followed every major story—the brief high of truth-telling followed by the crash of wondering if any of it mattered.

Eunice rubbed her eyes, smudging the minimal makeup she wore. At twenty-four, she was the youngest senior editor the Tribune had ever appointed, a fact that earned her equal parts admiration and resentment from the gray-haired men who still dominated the masthead. She'd built her reputation on meticulous research and an almost pathological inability to let a story go. Some called it dedication. Her ex-boyfriend had called it obsession before he'd packed his bags six months ago, tired of coming second to corruption scandals and freedom-of-information requests.

Her desk was a monument to controlled chaos—stacks of depositions, sticky notes with source phone numbers, three coffee cups in various states of abandonment. The only personal item was a small succulent her mother had given her, now brown and crispy from neglect. Eunice made a mental note to water it, then immediately forgot as she refreshed her email.

Threats. Praise. More threats. A marriage proposal from someone claiming to be a "truth warrior." The usual post-exposé circus.

But one email made her pause.

No subject line. No sender name, just a string of alphanumeric characters that her spam filter should have caught. The body contained a single line:

*For the woman who sees through smoke and mirrors.*

Eunice's finger hovered over the delete button. Probably some crank. Probably nothing. But something in the phrasing nagged at her—the specific choice of "smoke and mirrors," the formal address. She'd received plenty of anonymous tips over the years, and this had that same quality of deliberate mystery that usually meant someone with inside information was testing the waters.

"Mercer! You still here?"

She looked up to find Tom Wilkins, the night editor, pulling on his coat. The newsroom had emptied while she'd been lost in her screen. The wall clock read 9:47 PM.

"Just wrapping up," she lied.

"Go home. Get some sleep. Vane's lawyers are going to come at us hard tomorrow, and I need you sharp." Tom paused at her desk, his expression softening slightly. "Good work today. Really. You nailed that bastard."

Eunice managed a smile. "Thanks, Tom."

She waited until his footsteps faded down the stairwell before allowing her shoulders to slump. Home. Her apartment waited like a tomb—silent, dark, functional. She'd lived there for two years and still hadn't hung anything on the walls.

The elevator chimed as she gathered her things. Probably the cleaning crew. But when she looked up, she saw a courier standing in the newsroom entrance, holding a small package wrapped in brown paper.

"Eunice Mercer?" His voice was flat, bored. He wore no company uniform, just jeans and a black jacket.

"That's me."

He thrust a clipboard at her. "Sign here."

She scrawled her signature, curiosity prickling. She hadn't ordered anything.

The courier left without another word, and Eunice stood alone in the fluorescent wasteland of the newsroom, holding a package roughly the size of a hardcover book. No return address. No postage. Just her name in elegant calligraphy across the brown paper.

Her instincts screamed *suspicious package*, but her hands were already tearing the wrapping.

Inside was a case of brushed silver metal, its surface etched with art deco patterns—geometric flowers and sweeping lines that seemed to flow like smoke. The craftsmanship was exquisite, museum-quality. Eunice ran her fingers over the cool metal, feeling the subtle texture of the engravings.

A small card was tucked beneath the case.

*Congratulations on your latest revelation. Perhaps now you deserve a revelation of your own. —A.F.*

A.F. She mentally scrolled through her source list. No one with those initials. No one she'd interviewed for the Meridian story. The mystery deepened.

She should photograph it. Log it. Call someone. This was potentially evidence of... something. But evidence of what? A gift? An aesthetic object sent by an admirer?

The case had a small clasp on the front. It would take just a second to look inside.

Eunice's thumb pressed the release.

The case opened with a whisper-soft click, revealing twelve hand-rolled cigarettes nestled in individual velvet slots. They weren't ordinary cigarettes—the paper was cream-colored, almost ivory, and each one was wrapped with a thin gold band near the filter. The scent that rose from the case made her breath catch.

Jasmine. Burnt sugar. Something darker underneath, like sandalwood or aged leather.

She'd never smoked. Her father had died of lung cancer when she was seventeen, and she'd watched him deteriorate through eight months of chemotherapy that only bought him pain. She'd made a promise at his funeral, standing beside his closed casket because his body had wasted to something she didn't want to remember.

But these didn't smell like her father's Marlboros. These smelled like something from another era, something glamorous and dangerous. Like film noir made tangible.

Eunice lifted one cigarette from its velvet cradle. It was surprisingly light, delicate. The paper felt like silk between her fingers.

*Just to examine it*, she told herself. *Just to see what kind of person sends something like this.*

The cigarette seemed to warm in her hand. Probably just her body heat. Probably nothing.

There was a matchbook tucked into the case lid, also ivory, with the same art deco patterns. Her rational mind listed reasons not to do this: unknown substance, potential toxin, workplace fire codes. But her fingers struck the match anyway, and the sharp scent of phosphorus cut through the jasmine sweetness.

The flame wavered between her and the cigarette.

*One drag*, she thought. *Just to satisfy curiosity. That's what I do—I investigate things. This is investigation.*

The rationalization was paper-thin and she knew it, but her hand was already moving, bringing the cigarette to her lips, touching the tip to the flame.

The first drag was silk and smoke and something that tasted like honey mixed with burnt caramel. It rolled down her throat without the harshness she'd expected, without any of the acrid bite of normal tobacco. Instead, it felt almost... luxurious. Like breathing in velvet.

Eunice exhaled slowly, watching the smoke curl upward in the fluorescent light. It seemed thicker than normal cigarette smoke, heavier, and the patterns it made were almost hypnotic—spirals within spirals, fractals of gray that held her attention like a visual mantra.

She took another drag. And another.

By the fourth, her mind felt slightly soft around the edges, like the world had been wrapped in gauze. The anxiety that normally gnawed at her stomach had quieted. The fluorescent lights seemed less harsh. Even the dying succulent on her desk looked somehow romantic in its dessication.

The cigarette burned down to the gold band, and Eunice stubbed it out in an empty coffee cup, watching the ember hiss against the cold dregs.

She felt... different. Not high, exactly. Just altered. Like a photograph that had been slightly overexposed, making everything seem brighter and more significant.

The silver case sat open on her desk, eleven cigarettes remaining.

She should throw it away. She should report it to building security. She should at least take it home and put it in a drawer and forget about it.

Instead, Eunice Mercer closed the case with a silver click that seemed to echo in her chest, tucked it carefully into her bag, and walked toward the elevator with steady steps that didn't quite feel like her own.

### The Induction

# Chapter 2: The Silver Case

The cigarette case arrived on a Thursday.

Eunice found it on her desk at the *Tribune*, nestled between her coffee-stained notepad and the stack of affidavits she'd been using to dismantle Meridian Pharmaceutical's clinical trial fraud. No note. No return address. Just polished silver catching the fluorescent light, monogrammed with initials that weren't hers: *V.H.*

Victor Harrington.

The man she'd put behind bars three months ago was sending her gifts from prison.

She should have called security. Should have photographed it as evidence, documented the chain of custody like the good journalist she was. Instead, her fingers traced the engraved letters, feeling the craftsman's grooves. The case was cool against her palm, surprisingly heavy. Quality. The kind of object that demanded to be opened.

*Don't.*

But she was already lifting the lid.

Six cigarettes lay in perfect parallel rows, their paper pristine white, filters tipped with gold leaf. They looked hand-rolled, artisanal. The tobacco scent that escaped was nothing like the convenience store brands—this was rich, dark, complex. Notes of cherry and vanilla and something else she couldn't quite identify. Something that made her mouth water despite never having smoked in her life.

A slip of paper lay beneath them, cream-colored card stock with embossed lettering:

*For the discerning palate. One is an invitation. Six is a conversation.*

No signature. None needed.

Eunice's rational mind catalogued the warning signs: unsolicited gift, convicted felon, possible tampering. She'd written seventeen articles about Meridian's misconduct. She knew exactly what Victor Harrington was capable of—the falsified data, the buried side effects, the patients who'd trusted his compounds and paid with their sanity.

This was a message. A threat disguised as elegance.

She should photograph it. Document it. Turn it over to—

Her fingers had already selected a cigarette.

The paper felt like silk between her fingers, the weight of it perfectly balanced. She'd interviewed enough reformed addicts to understand the ritualistic appeal, the hand-to-mouth motion that became meditation. But understanding intellectually and feeling the rightness of it settling into her palm were entirely different things.

*Just one. To analyze it. To understand what he's planning.*

The rationalization came so smoothly she almost believed it.

Her desk lighter—a promotional item from some pharmaceutical conference she'd covered—flared to life. The flame kissed the cigarette's tip, and the tobacco caught with a soft crackle. Eunice drew breath through the filter, expecting harshness, expecting to cough, expecting her virgin lungs to rebel.

Instead: velvet.

The smoke poured into her mouth like cream, impossibly smooth, tasting of burnt sugar and midnight promises. It shouldn't have been possible to inhale so deeply on a first attempt, but her lungs opened like they'd been waiting for this their entire life. The smoke filled her chest, warm and complete, and when she exhaled, the plume twisted upward in lazy spirals that seemed almost geometric in their precision.

*Oh.*

The word formed somewhere behind her sternum, too deep to speak.

The second drag was instinct. The smoke patterns bloomed in front of her face, and Eunice found herself staring into them, watching the gray tendrils curl and reform. They almost looked like letters. Like a message written in vapor, dissolving even as her brain tried to parse the meaning.

Her scalp tingled. A pleasant buzz, like good champagne, but centered somehow. Focused. The fluorescent lights overhead seemed less harsh suddenly. The hum of the newsroom—phones ringing, keyboards clattering, voices overlapping—became a comfortable murmur instead of an assault.

She could think more clearly. That was the strange part. Her thoughts weren't fuzzy or impaired; they were *crystalline*. Sharp-edged and gleaming. The affidavits on her desk practically glowed with relevance. She could see connections she'd missed before, patterns in the testimonies that formed a narrative so obvious she wanted to laugh at her previous blindness.

Another drag. The smoke tasted better each time, her body learning the rhythm. Inhale. Hold. Exhale. Watch the patterns. Feel the warmth spread from her lungs outward, loosening muscles she hadn't realized were tense.

When had she last relaxed?

The question floated past like the smoke, interesting but not urgent. Eunice reached for her keyboard, cigarette balanced between the first and second fingers of her right hand, and began typing. The words flowed faster than they had in weeks. Months. Her usual perfectionism—the constant second-guessing, the delete-and-retype cycle—had evaporated. She knew what to say. The truth arranged itself in perfect sentences, ready to be harvested.

She was halfway through a new article on pharmaceutical oversight reform when she realized the cigarette had burned down to the filter.

The loss hit her like grief.

Eunice stared at the spent filter, still warm between her fingers, and felt something twist in her chest. The clarity was fading already. The sharp edges of her thoughts softening back into their usual rounded anxiety. The newsroom sounds grew harsh again, discordant. The lights too bright.

Her eyes found the silver case.

*Five left.*

The rational journalist in her mind—the woman who'd built a career on skepticism and evidence—whispered warnings about chemical dependency, about neurological hijacking, about Victor Harrington's documented expertise in addictive compounds.

But that voice sounded far away. Muffled.

What she could hear, crystal clear, was the memory of that first perfect drag. The way her lungs had welcomed the smoke like a homecoming. The precision of her thoughts while the cigarette smoldered. The *rightness* of the ritual.

Her fingers were already reaching for the second cigarette before she'd consciously decided.

This time she noticed details she'd missed: the way the gold filter caught the light, the perfect uniformity of the paper's grain, the subtle sweetness that lingered on her lips after exhaling. The smoke patterns seemed more complex now, almost hypnotic in their geometry. She found herself staring into them, watching the shapes form and dissolve, form and dissolve, while that pleasant warmth spread through her chest like dark honey.

When had her office gotten so cold? The smoke was the only warm thing in the room.

Eunice worked through the afternoon with the cigarette burning between her fingers, pausing only to ash into her coffee mug when her official ashtray (non-existent, because she'd never needed one) proved unavailable. The bitter scent of burnt coffee and ash should have been repulsive. Instead it smelled *productive*. It smelled like work getting done.

By the time her editor stopped by at five, she'd finished three articles and started researching a fourth.

"Christ, Mercer, when did you start smoking?" Brian's nose wrinkled, his hand waving theatrically at the haze hanging around her desk.

When had she lit the third one?

Eunice looked down at her hand—at the cigarette burning between fingers that now seemed perfectly adapted to holding it—and felt a flutter of something that might have been concern. But it was distant. Theoretical. Like worrying about a stranger's problems.

"Research," she said, and was surprised by how husky her voice had become. Throaty. She sounded like late-night radio, like whiskey and dark rooms.

She sounded sexy.

The thought arrived fully formed and completely inappropriate. Eunice Mercer didn't think about being sexy. She thought about sourcing, about verification, about the public's right to know. But now her brain was offering her an image: herself in the bathroom mirror, cigarette dangling from lips painted darker than her usual nude gloss, eyes narrowed against the smoke, looking dangerous and knowing and—

"Whatever." Brian dropped a file on her desk, then hesitated. "You feeling okay? You look... different."

"I feel productive," she said, and meant it. The cigarette between her fingers pulsed with warmth, an anchor point in the chaos. "I feel *clear*."

After he left, she allowed herself to examine that clarity. It was still there, sharp as broken glass, showing her truths she'd been too cluttered to see before. The affidavits weren't just evidence of fraud—they were maps to power structures. Networks of influence. Weak points in armor that could be exploited for...

For what?

The thought skittered away before she could examine it. Eunice took another drag, letting the smoke fill her lungs until they ached pleasantly, and watched the patterns bloom in the air. There was definitely a message in them. A design. If she could just focus hard enough, she could read what the smoke was telling her.

Her phone buzzed. Unknown number. She let it go to voicemail.

The newsroom emptied around her as evening crept across the windows. The night shift drifted in—copy editors, web designers, the skeleton crew that kept a newspaper alive while the city slept. They gave her desk a wide berth. Maybe it was the smoke. Maybe it was something in her expression when she glanced up from her screen, cigarette smoldering, seeing them as interruptions rather than colleagues.

When had that shift happened?

By midnight, she'd smoked all six cigarettes.

The silver case lay empty on her desk, its velvet interior marked with ash she didn't remember dropping. Eunice's fingers felt strange without a cigarette between them—naked and incomplete. Her mouth tasted of burnt vanilla and promises. Her lungs felt coated in warmth that was already dissipating, leaving her cold.

She needed another.

The craving arrived without warning, a full-body *need* that made her hands shake. This wasn't the gentle pull of habit. This was hunger. Desperate and immediate and completely irrational considering she'd never wanted a cigarette in her life until eight hours ago.

*What did he put in them?*

The question should have terrified her. Should have sent her running to a hospital, demanding blood tests, documenting the symptoms. But her brain—that crystalline, efficient engine that had been purring all afternoon—offered a different interpretation:

*What does it matter? You've never felt this good.*

And that was true, wasn't it? The anxiety that usually jacketed her thoughts had vanished. The imposter syndrome that whispered she wasn't smart enough, wasn't ruthless enough to make it in investigative journalism—gone. She'd written better copy in six hours than she usually managed in a week.

She'd felt *powerful*.

Eunice picked up the empty silver case, turning it over in her hands. The monogram seemed to shimmer in the desk lamp light: V.H. Victor Harrington. The man she'd destroyed. The man who was, apparently, rebuilding her from the inside out.

Her phone buzzed again. Same unknown number. This time she answered.

"Did you enjoy them?" The voice was cultured, amused. Not Victor Harrington's voice—she'd memorized his testimony—but someone speaking for him. "There's a package waiting at your apartment. Twenty-four more. Consider it... an investment."

The line went dead.

Eunice sat in the empty newsroom, empty silver case in one hand, phone in the other, and felt something shift in her chest. A fault line cracking. On one side: the journalist who'd built her career on exposing men like Victor Harrington. On the other: a woman who could still taste burnt vanilla on her tongue and would sell her credentials for another cigarette.

She should document this. Call the police. Write the article that would expose whatever compound he'd laced them with.

Instead, she found herself gathering her things, slipping the silver case into her purse, walking toward the elevator with legs that felt steadier than they had in years.

There was a package waiting. Twenty-four cigarettes.

She could analyze them. Study them. Understand the methodology before deciding whether to—

*Who are you lying to?*

The thought came in her own voice, but colder. More honest.

Eunice jabbed the elevator button and didn't answer.

In the polished doors, her reflection looked back: lipstick slightly smudged, eyes brighter than usual, posture different somehow. More aware of her own body. Her tongue touched her lower lip, tasting the memory of smoke, and she noticed—really noticed—the gesture's inherent sexuality.

When had she started moving like this?

The elevator arrived. She stepped inside. Behind her, the newsroom where she'd built her reputation sat dark and empty, filled with stories about truth and justice and accountability.

Eunice watched her reflection in the closing doors and thought about cigarettes.

Twenty-four of them, waiting in her apartment.

It would be enough for three days. Maybe four if she rationed them. And then...

And then she'd need more.

The certainty settled into her bones like smoke into lungs: perfect, inevitable, already complete.

# Chapter Three

The cigarette case sat on Eunice's desk like an accusation.

Three days since she'd found it in her coat pocket—that sleek silver thing with no identifying marks, no note, just twelve ivory cigarettes nestled in cream silk. Three days since she'd smoked the first one outside the office, telling herself it was just curiosity, just research for the exposé she wasn't writing anymore.

The pharmaceutical CEO had been her Pulitzer shot—a monster who'd pushed opioids into working-class neighborhoods while sitting on addiction research. Her hand drifted toward the drawer where she'd hidden the case.

*No.*

She jerked her fingers back, curling them into a fist. She'd promised herself—one more at lunch, not before. The headache that had been building since yesterday intensified, a vise tightening behind her eyes. Her throat felt raw, tight.

"Mercer, you got a minute?"

Dale stood in her doorway, rumpled shirt and coffee-stained tie marking him as her managing editor. The man who'd mentored her through her first investigative piece, who'd fought the publisher to give her the Vandermeer story.

"The draft was due yesterday," he said, not unkindly.

"I know. I'm—" Eunice's voice came out hoarse. She cleared her throat. "Technical issues with the sources. Give me until tomorrow."

"Fine. Just need coffee."

*You're burning yourself out over nothing.*

She took another drag.

The smoke seemed thicker this time, almost visible as it entered her body. She could feel it in her veins, could sense pathways lighting up in her brain like a transit map. Dopamine flooding receptor sites. Neural connections strengthening, rewiring.

*What you need is a real story. Something with teeth.*

*Like the mayor's affair. The photos are right there, easy access through that source at the hotel. Sexy, scandalous, guaranteed front page.*

*It's everyone's kind of story. You're not special for suffering through boring financial documents. Work smarter.*

The cigarette had burned down to the filter. Eunice crushed it beneath her heel, watching the ember die. Her reflection caught in the stairwell door's window—she looked the same as always. Brown hair pulled back, minimal makeup, the practical blazer she'd worn for three years.

So why did she feel like someone else was looking back?

Inside, she made it halfway to her desk before the craving returned. Sharper this time, almost painful. Her fingers trembled as she gripped her coffee mug. The Vandermeer file sat open on her screen, cursor still blinking.

She should write. She should push through this bizarre fog and finish the piece that would make her career.

Instead, Eunice found herself opening a new document. Her fingers moved across the keyboard with surprising speed, typing a pitch for the mayor's affair story. The words flowed easily, naturally. She outlined the sources, the access points, the human interest angle that would sell papers.

When she finished, she read it back and felt nothing but satisfaction.

Dale would love this. A guaranteed viral story, the kind that drove subscriptions. And it would take maybe two weeks to report versus the months she'd already sunk into Vandermeer.

*Efficient. Smart. This is what real journalists do.*

Eunice's hand drifted to the drawer again. Four hours until lunch, but the need was building. Chemical and undeniable.

Just one more couldn't hurt.

### The Cognitive Dissonance

# Chapter 4: Smoke and Mirrors

The letter from the **Chronicle's** legal department sat on Eunice's desk like a accusation. Three fact-checking errors in her latest article. Three. In six years of journalism, she'd never had more than one correction, and that had been a misattributed quote.

She read the highlighted passages again, her hand trembling slightly as she reached for the silver cigarette case. It had become automatic now—stress meant smoke, and lately there was nothing but stress.

*"...pharmaceutical executive Marcus Thorne allegedly..."*

Allegedly. She'd written "definitely." The distinction mattered in journalism. How had she missed that?

*"...seventeen complaints filed with the FDA..."*

Seventeen. Her notes said nineteen. She'd been so certain.

The cigarette slid between her lips, and relief flooded through her even before she lit it. That first drag pulled tight in her chest, the smoke curling down her throat like a lover's whisper. The nicotine hit her bloodstream, and the world softened at its edges.

Better. Everything was better with smoke in her lungs.

"Mercer." Frank stood in her doorway, his expression carefully neutral. "We need to talk about the Henderson piece."

She exhaled slowly, watching the smoke drift between them. "The sources were solid."

"The sources recanted." He stepped inside, closing the door. "Three of them called yesterday. Said you misrepresented their statements."

That couldn't be right. She'd recorded the interviews. Except... when had she last reviewed the recordings? She could remember the conversations clearly—couldn't she? Henderson's nervous laugh. The way he'd tapped his pen against the conference table. The admission that he'd falsified the clinical trial data.

Or had that been someone else? Another interview, another story?

The cigarette burned between her fingers. She brought it back to her lips, needing the clarity that smoke provided. The ember glowed bright red as she inhaled, and for a moment the confusion lifted.

*It doesn't matter,* something whispered in her mind. *The truth isn't what matters.*

Eunice blinked. Where had that thought come from?

"I'll review the tapes," she said, but her voice sounded distant to her own ears.

Frank studied her with concerned eyes. "Are you okay? You've been different lately. Distracted."

"I'm fine." The cigarette had burned halfway down. She watched the ash lengthen, that delicate gray cylinder that would collapse at the slightest tremor. Fragile. Temporary. Like certainty itself.

She ashed into the crystal tray—when had she brought that from home?—and the motion felt ritualistic. Sacred, almost.

"Take the rest of the day," Frank said. "Get some air. Clear your head."

But air was the last thing she wanted. Air was empty. Air didn't fill the hollow space that seemed to be expanding inside her chest, that aching void that only smoke could satisfy.

After he left, Eunice tried to work. She pulled up her notes on the Thorne investigation—the exposé that had made her career, that had sent the pharmaceutical CEO to prison for illegally testing his neurochemical compounds on unknowing subjects.

The words swam on her screen. She lit another cigarette, and the smoke helped her focus, but not on the work. Instead she found herself staring at the photograph embedded in the file. Marcus Thorne, being led from the courthouse in handcuffs. Dark hair threaded with silver. Sharp cheekbones. Eyes that seemed to look directly at her through the pixels, amused and knowing.

*You're becoming what you despised,* she thought, but the observation felt clinical rather than alarming. *A manipulator. A liar.*

The cigarette smoke curled around her fingers, caressing her wrist. She brought it back to her lips and pulled deep, feeling the heat and bite of it, the way it filled her completely.

And god help her, it felt good.

Better than good. It felt *right*.

The realization should have terrified her. Instead, a languid warmth spread through her body, pooling low in her belly. She squeezed her thighs together beneath the desk, shocked by the sudden pulse of arousal.

She'd open it again in ten minutes.

She was happy. Finally, perfectly, emptily happy.

And she would never fight again.
